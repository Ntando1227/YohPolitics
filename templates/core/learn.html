Yoh, Politics breaks down South African politics without turning it into a boring textbook. The goal is simple: understand the rules, understand the parties, understand the promises, and understand how power affects everyday life.

01

How South Africa’s political system works

South Africa is a constitutional democracy. This means the Constitution is the highest law, and government cannot legally act above it.

What this means

Political power is limited by law. The President, Parliament, courts, provinces, municipalities, and public officials must operate within the Constitution.

Why it matters

Without constitutional limits, political power can easily become abuse. The Constitution protects citizens from government overreach.

Example: If Parliament passes a law that violates constitutional rights, the Constitutional Court can strike it down.
02

Parliament: more than political drama

Parliament is where laws are debated, budgets are approved, ministers are questioned, and the executive is held accountable.

Main roles

Parliament makes laws, oversees government departments, approves national spending, debates national issues, and represents voters through political parties.

Why people misunderstand it

Many people only see Parliament when there is shouting or scandal. Committee work, budget oversight, and law-making are where serious work happens.

Yoh, Politics view: If Parliament becomes weak, ministers become harder to hold accountable.
03

Proportional representation

South Africa uses proportional representation for national and provincial elections. Parties get seats based on their share of the vote.

The benefit

Smaller parties can win seats even if they do not win a whole geographic area. This makes Parliament more representative.

The weakness

Voters often vote for party lists instead of directly choosing individual representatives. This can make politicians more loyal to party leadership than voters.

Simple version: If a party gets roughly 10% of the vote, it should get roughly 10% of the seats.
04

South Africa’s 3-ballot voting system

South Africa now uses a three-ballot system during national and provincial elections. This was introduced to allow independent candidates to compete more directly.

Ballot 1: National Parties Ballot

This ballot is for political parties competing nationally. It helps decide the overall balance of power in the National Assembly.

Ballot 2: Regional Ballot

This ballot lets voters choose parties or independent candidates representing their province at national level.

Ballot 3: Provincial Legislature Ballot

This ballot determines who governs your province. Provinces influence healthcare, education, housing, roads, and provincial planning.

Why this matters

Voters must think about who they trust nationally, provincially, and whether they prefer party representation or independent candidates.

Yoh, Politics reading: The 3-ballot system gives voters more choices, but it also makes coalition politics and representation more complicated.
05

Coalition governments

A coalition happens when no single party has enough seats to govern alone, so parties negotiate to govern together.

Why coalitions happen

When voters split support across many parties, no party may win a majority.

The risk

Coalitions can collapse if parties fight over positions, tenders, policy disagreements, or leadership ambitions.

Political tension: Coalitions can improve accountability, but they can also create instability.
06

Government of National Unity

A Government of National Unity is a broad governing arrangement where multiple parties cooperate inside government.

Why parties join it

Parties may join to create stability, influence policy, access cabinet positions, or prevent another bloc from taking control.

Why it can be tense

Parties in the same government may still disagree strongly on land, labour, crime, corruption, grants, and economic reform.

Key question: Is the GNU democratic maturity, or a temporary compromise between parties that do not fully trust each other?
07

National, provincial, and local government

South Africa has three spheres of government: national, provincial, and local. They are supposed to be distinct but cooperative.

National

Handles major laws, national policy, defence, police, taxation, foreign affairs, and national departments.

Provincial

Plays a major role in education, healthcare, provincial roads, and provincial planning.

Local

Deals with water, sanitation, refuse removal, municipal roads, electricity distribution, and local services.

Why local government matters: Many people judge politics through water, roads, rubbish collection, streetlights, and billing.
08

BEE and transformation

BEE stands for Black Economic Empowerment. It is part of South Africa’s attempt to correct apartheid’s economic legacy.

Supporters argue

Apartheid deliberately excluded black South Africans from ownership, management, education, land, and opportunity.

Critics argue

BEE has sometimes benefited politically connected elites more than ordinary people.

The real debate: How do you correct historical injustice without creating corruption, tokenism, or elite enrichment?
09

Land reform

Land reform is about correcting land dispossession caused by colonialism and apartheid.

Why it matters

Land is connected to dignity, housing, farming, wealth, family history, food security, and economic power.

Why it is controversial

Land reform raises questions about property rights, investor confidence, food production, historical justice, and state capacity.

Yoh, Politics view: Land is never only about land. It is about memory, ownership, belonging, and power.
10

Cadre deployment

Cadre deployment is when a political party places loyal members or allies into public positions.

Supporters may say

A governing party needs people who understand its political programme and can help implement it.

Critics say

It weakens the state when loyalty becomes more important than competence, experience, ethics, and skill.

Why it matters: If the wrong people run key institutions, service delivery, corruption control, and public trust suffer.
11

Left wing, right wing, and South African ideology

In South Africa, left and right do not work exactly like American or European politics.

Left-leaning politics often focuses on

Redistribution, land reform, worker rights, public services, state intervention, equality, and transformation.

Right-leaning politics often focuses on

Property rights, markets, law and order, lower state interference, tradition, and individual responsibility.

Important: Many South African parties mix left and right positions in complicated ways.
