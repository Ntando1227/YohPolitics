Learn Politics Properly

Politics is not only about parties and elections. It is about power, resources, institutions, identity, history, public money, law, conflict, and the everyday decisions that shape how people live.

How to read South African politics

South African politics cannot be understood only by asking who is popular, who is corrupt, or who gives the best speech. You need to ask deeper questions: who holds power, who controls resources, whose suffering becomes visible, whose interests are protected, and which institutions are strong enough to turn promises into reality.

This page gives you the basic political tools needed to understand parties, manifestos, public debates, elections, coalitions, ideology, policy and the state.

01

What politics actually is

Politics is the organised struggle over how society should be governed. It decides who gets what, who pays for what, who is protected, who is punished, whose voice matters, and what kind of future a country is trying to build.

Power

Power is the ability to make decisions that affect other people. It can sit in government, business, courts, communities, unions, traditional leadership, media, churches, families and social movements.

Resources

Politics is also about money, land, jobs, electricity, water, housing, healthcare and education. When resources are scarce or unequal, politics becomes more intense.

Institutions

Institutions are the rules and systems that organise public life: Parliament, courts, municipalities, schools, hospitals, police, Home Affairs, SARS, Eskom and the IEC.

Conflict

Conflict is normal in politics. The question is whether society has democratic institutions strong enough to manage disagreement without violence, collapse or permanent distrust.

02

The state, government and the ruling party are not the same thing

One of the biggest mistakes people make is treating the state, government and the ruling party as if they are the same. They are connected, but they are not identical.

The state

The state is the permanent machinery of public authority. It includes departments, courts, police, public schools, hospitals, municipalities, regulators and public entities. The state should serve the public, not one party.

The government

The government is the group currently elected or appointed to lead the state. Governments change after elections, but the state should continue functioning.

The ruling party

The ruling party is the political organisation that wins enough power to form government. It has ideology, interests, factions, voters and internal battles.

State capture

State capture happens when public institutions are redirected away from the public interest and toward private, factional or corrupt interests.

A healthy democracy needs a capable state that can survive party politics. If every institution becomes a party battlefield, public administration weakens and citizens lose trust.
03

How democracy works beyond voting

Elections are important, but democracy is bigger than voting once every few years. Democracy also requires accountability, rights, participation, independent courts, free media, public debate and institutions that obey the Constitution.

Representation

Citizens choose representatives to make decisions on their behalf. Those representatives should remain answerable to the public.

Accountability

Leaders must explain decisions, answer questions, face consequences for misconduct and be removable through democratic processes.

Participation

Democracy includes voting, petitions, public hearings, protest, community meetings, journalism, civil society work and court action.

Rights

Rights protect citizens from abuse by the state and by powerful interests. They also create duties for government to progressively improve people’s lives.

A country can hold elections and still have weak democracy if corruption is normal, institutions are intimidated, public money is abused and citizens cannot hold leaders accountable.
04

The South African three-ballot system

South Africa’s national and provincial elections use three ballots. This matters because your vote is not just one simple action. You are helping shape national Parliament, provincial government and representation by parties or independent candidates.

National ballot

This ballot is for political parties contesting nationally. It helps decide the national composition of Parliament and therefore who can form national government.

Regional ballot

This ballot is also for the National Assembly, but it is linked to your province. It allows voters to choose parties or qualifying independent candidates contesting for regional seats.

Provincial ballot

This ballot decides representation in your provincial legislature. It shapes provincial leadership, provincial budgets, education, health, roads and provincial governance.

Why it matters

You are not only choosing a president indirectly. You are shaping different layers of power. National and provincial politics can produce different results and different coalition possibilities.

Do not treat all three ballots casually. A voter may support one party nationally, another provincially, or make a strategic choice depending on coalitions, local performance and policy priorities.
05

Ideology: the story a party tells about society

Ideology is not just a label like left-wing or right-wing. It is the deeper story a party tells about what is wrong with society, who caused the problem, who should fix it and what a better country should look like.

Left politics

Usually emphasises inequality, workers, redistribution, public services, state intervention and structural injustice.

Right politics

Usually emphasises markets, order, private property, tradition, individual responsibility, security and limits on state power.

Centre politics

Usually tries to balance market growth, social support, institutional stability and gradual reform.

Populism

Populism frames politics as a battle between “the people” and corrupt elites. It can appear on the left or the right.

In South Africa, ideology is shaped by apartheid history, race, land, inequality, unemployment, corruption, crime, public services and the memory of liberation.
06

Manifestos: promises, priorities and political imagination

A manifesto is not only a list of promises. It is a document that reveals what a party thinks the country’s biggest problems are, who it is trying to speak to, and what kind of society it wants to create.

Promises

These are the things a party says it will do, such as creating jobs, reducing crime, improving education or reforming healthcare.

Priorities

What a party repeats most tells you what it thinks matters politically. Repetition is a signal.

Silences

What a manifesto avoids can be just as important as what it says. Silence can reveal political caution or weak policy thinking.

Feasibility

The key question is not only whether the promise sounds good. The question is whether money, institutions and political conditions make it possible.

A serious voter asks: What is promised? Who benefits? Who pays? What institution must implement it? What could go wrong?
07

Policy: how political ideas become real life

Policy is where political vision meets practical reality. It is the plan, law, budget, programme or regulation that turns an idea into action.

Policy goal

What problem is the policy trying to solve? A weak diagnosis often leads to a weak solution.

Policy instrument

Is government using grants, tax, regulation, policing, public ownership, incentives, procurement or infrastructure?

Implementation

Who must actually do the work? A department, municipality, school, hospital, police station or agency?

Evaluation

Did the policy work? Did it help the intended people? Did it create new problems?

Many policies fail not because the idea is morally wrong, but because the state lacks capacity, coordination, money, honesty or technical expertise.
08

Coalitions: compromise, instability and bargaining

Coalitions happen when no party wins enough power to govern alone. Parties must negotiate to form a government, pass budgets and distribute positions.

Policy compromise

Coalition partners often disagree. They must decide which promises survive and which ones are watered down.

Position bargaining

Parties negotiate mayoral posts, committee chairs, cabinet positions and control over departments.

Instability

If trust is weak, coalitions can collapse repeatedly. This affects service delivery and long-term planning.

Accountability problems

When many parties govern together, it becomes harder for voters to know who to blame or reward.

Coalition politics can deepen democracy through negotiation, but it can also become unstable if parties care more about positions than public governance.
09

State capacity: the difference between promise and delivery

State capacity is one of the most important ideas in politics. It means the ability of public institutions to actually do what government promises.

Administrative capacity

Can departments plan, coordinate, hire skilled people, keep records and run programmes properly?

Fiscal capacity

Can the state raise and manage enough money to fund its commitments?

Technical capacity

Does government have engineers, doctors, planners, data specialists, teachers, prosecutors and project managers?

Moral capacity

Are institutions protected from corruption, patronage and political abuse?

A weak state can ruin both socialist and market-oriented policies. Without capacity, even good ideas become broken systems.
10

How to judge a political party

Do not judge a party only by whether you like its leader or slogan. Judge it by its worldview, policy logic, institutional seriousness and relationship to power.

Diagnosis

What does the party think South Africa’s real problem is?

Solution

Does its proposed solution match the problem it identified?

Capacity

Has the party shown it can govern, organise, administer or build institutions?

Trade-offs

Every policy has winners, losers, costs and risks. Serious parties admit this.

The best political question is not “Which party sounds perfect?” It is “Which party’s diagnosis, values, risks and governing ability make the most sense?”

11

Political economy: why economics and politics cannot be separated

Political economy is the study of how power and economics interact. It asks who owns wealth, who controls production, who benefits from policy, and how economic systems shape political behaviour.

In South Africa, political economy matters enormously because apartheid was not only a racial system. It was also an economic system. Race determined access to land, jobs, education, ownership, movement, housing and opportunity.

Markets and power

Markets are never completely neutral. Governments create laws, regulate trade, tax wealth, issue licences, protect property, manage labour systems and influence investment.

Inequality

South Africa is one of the most unequal societies in the world. Inequality shapes crime, schooling, health outcomes, political anger, trust and economic mobility.

Capital

Capital refers to money, assets, businesses, infrastructure and investment power. Political debates often revolve around whether capital is being regulated properly or protected excessively.

Labour

Labour refers to workers and employment systems. Wage struggles, strikes, unemployment and labour law are central political questions in South Africa.

Political economy asks a deeper question than “Is the economy growing?” It asks: Who benefits from the growth? Who remains excluded? And what political consequences emerge from inequality?
12

Why corruption destroys states

Corruption is not simply theft. It weakens the ability of institutions to function, destroys public trust, distorts policy decisions and changes how citizens relate to democracy itself.

In highly unequal societies, corruption becomes especially dangerous because citizens already depend heavily on public systems for housing, healthcare, transport, education and social support.

Institutional decay

When appointments are made for loyalty rather than competence, institutions lose technical expertise and long-term stability.

Public distrust

Citizens stop believing government promises. This weakens democratic legitimacy and increases political anger.

Economic damage

Corruption increases costs, discourages investment, weakens infrastructure and redirects public money away from developmental priorities.

Political cynicism

Over time, people begin assuming corruption is normal. This normalisation is extremely damaging for democracy.

One of the greatest dangers in politics is when citizens stop asking “How do we stop corruption?” and start asking “Is honest government even possible?”
13

Why unemployment changes politics

Mass unemployment does not only create economic problems. It transforms political behaviour, social trust, public anger and national psychology.

Young people without stable work often experience delayed adulthood, dependency, humiliation, uncertainty and emotional exhaustion. These conditions reshape politics.

Populism

High unemployment creates fertile ground for emotionally powerful politics. Citizens become more willing to support parties promising dramatic solutions.

Social instability

Long-term exclusion weakens social cohesion. Crime, frustration, protest and distrust can intensify.

Migration politics

When jobs are scarce, migration debates become more emotionally charged. Economic fear often reshapes attitudes toward outsiders.

Democratic fatigue

Citizens who repeatedly experience economic disappointment may begin losing faith in democratic institutions themselves.

Unemployment is not only a labour-market issue. It is one of the most important political stability questions in South Africa.
14

Nationalism, identity and belonging

Politics is emotional because people want to belong. National identity shapes how citizens understand citizenship, borders, migration, language, race, culture and public memory.

In South Africa, identity politics remains powerful because the country’s history deeply shaped who had access to dignity, safety and opportunity.

Nationalism

Nationalism is the belief that citizens share a collective political identity. It can unify societies, but it can also become exclusionary.

Race and memory

Historical memory still shapes political language, land debates, inequality discussions and institutional trust.

Migration

Migration politics becomes intense when economic insecurity rises. Governments must balance border enforcement with human rights.

Social cohesion

Democracies require enough shared identity for citizens to cooperate despite disagreement.

Identity politics becomes more emotionally powerful when economic systems fail to create stability and inclusion.
15

Why states collapse slowly before they collapse publicly

States rarely collapse all at once. Institutional decline usually happens gradually: weakened administration, loss of skills, procurement failure, infrastructure decay, corruption, political interference and declining public trust.

Citizens often notice symptoms before elites acknowledge the structural crisis.

Administrative weakening

Departments lose institutional memory, technical skills and operational coordination.

Infrastructure decline

Roads, electricity, rail, water systems and municipalities become harder to maintain consistently.

Legitimacy crisis

Citizens stop trusting institutions to solve problems effectively.

Political fragmentation

As trust weakens, political systems become more fragmented, emotional and unstable.

The most dangerous political moment is not when citizens are angry. It is when citizens stop believing institutions can improve at all.
