Campus Politics

Student politics is not separate from national politics. NSFAS, protests, unemployment, accommodation, safety, identity, and activism all shape how young people experience democracy.

Youth and Campus Life

Campus is where the future meets the state directly.

For many young South Africans, politics becomes real through funding delays, registration blocks, food insecurity, accommodation stress, protests, and graduate anxiety.

01

NSFAS and the politics of access

NSFAS is not only a funding scheme. It is one of the biggest gateways between poverty and higher education.

Why it matters

When NSFAS works, poor and working-class students can enter higher education. When it fails, students face hunger, debt, exclusion, and emotional pressure.

The political issue

Funding delays reveal state-capacity problems: administration, data, payment systems, fraud controls, university coordination, and budget pressure.

Yoh, Politics view: Free education is incomplete if students are funded on paper but hungry, homeless, or blocked in practice.
02

Student protests

Student protests often happen when formal systems fail to resolve urgent problems.

What students protest about

Fees, accommodation, registration, debt, NSFAS, racism, safety, food insecurity, academic exclusion, and institutional accountability.

Why it becomes emotional

Students are not only fighting for services. They are fighting for futures that can disappear because of one unpaid fee, one missing allowance, or one admin failure.

Political tension: Protests can disrupt learning, but they often expose problems institutions ignored for too long.
03

Graduate anxiety

Graduate anxiety is the fear that education no longer guarantees opportunity.

Why it happens

High unemployment, weak networks, unpaid internships, transport costs, experience requirements, and slow hiring make the transition from campus to work painful.

Why it is political

The education system, economy, labour market, public investment, and private-sector confidence all shape whether graduates find work.

Hard truth: A degree can improve chances, but it does not automatically overcome a broken economy.
04

Political apathy on campus

Political apathy is not always laziness. Sometimes it is disappointment.

Why students disengage

They may feel parties use youth for rallies but ignore their real problems: jobs, rent, data costs, transport, mental health, and safety.

Why it matters

If young people stop believing politics can change anything, democracy becomes older, less energetic, and less responsive.

Yoh, Politics view: Young people are not politically empty. Many are politically tired.
05

Youth activism

Youth activism is how young people force institutions to respond when normal channels feel too slow.

Where activism happens

SRCs, protests, petitions, social media campaigns, community projects, student organisations, and issue-based movements.

The risk

Activism can become performative if it becomes only slogans, clout, or factional politics.

Best version: Youth activism is strongest when it combines anger, evidence, organisation, and a clear demand.
